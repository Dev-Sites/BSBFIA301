SECTION 1 Projecting financial requirements ▽

    Personal financial situation ▽

      Before you take the step of starting up your own micro-business, you need to carefully assess your personal financial situation and analyse your financial readiness. This will help you determine your suitability to start and run a business and allow you to provide valuable information to stakeholders providing investment capital for your venture.

      To assess your personal financial situation, you should:

      • Prepare your personal balance sheet, and
      • Develop a personal budget.

      Personal Balance Sheet

      The following steps will help you prepare your personal balance sheet:

      1) Estimate the market value of your personal assets including cash and cash convertibles, property, vehicles, home furnishings, collectibles, jewellery, etc.

      2) Add up the value of all these assets.

      3) List all of your liabilities including mortgage, personal loans, credit cards, legal and regulatory obligations, etc.

      4) Add up all your liabilities.

      5) Subtract your total liabilities from total assets to determine your net worth.

      Personal Budget

      The following steps will help you develop your personal budget:

      1) List all income sources that are available to you including salary or wages, social security benefits, investment income, etc.

      2) Add up all your income from these sources.

      3) List all of your expenses, including:

      a) Fixed expenses – mortgage and loan repayments, rent, insurance premiums, property and income taxes, etc.

      b) Variable expenses – food and clothing, medical expenses, utilities, transportation, repairs and maintenance, etc.

      c) Other expenses – travel, entertainment, hobbies, money required to build future security (for example savings, retirement plans, investments, etc.), drawings required to support personal/ family/ community commitments, etc.

      4) Add up all your expenses.

      5) Subtract your total expense from total income to find out your personal financial needs.

    Financial needs ▽

      Identifying all the potential costs you will face is a crucial first step in starting a business. These costs might include:

      Start-up costs – the initial one-off outlays when starting your business and can include expenses such as:

      • Security bond and initial rent for business premises.
      • Repairs, remodelling and redecorating of business premises.
      • Equipment/ tools/ machinery purchases – including installation costs.
      • Security deposits for utilities, telephones, leased or rented equipment.
      • Furniture and fixtures.
      • Office supplies and stationary.
      • Vehicles.
      • Initial stock.
      • Pre-opening marketing, advertising and promotion costs.
      • Licenses, registrations, and permits.
      • Legal and professional fees.

      Ongoing costs – the continual costs required to run and maintain the business and can include expenses such as:

      • Rent and lease payments.
      • Mortgage and loan repayments.
      • Phone and utilities.
      • Maintenance and repair of premises, equipment, and vehicles.
      • Office supplies and stationary.
      • Operating stock/ suppliers bills.
      • Advertising and promotion.
      • Delivery and shipping costs.
      • Personal drawings and employee wages.
      • Insurance premiums.
      • Taxes.
      • Membership fees and subscriptions.

      It is also very important to consider including an allowance or “float” as working capital for the early stages where you may not necessarily generate enough revenue to cover all your costs. Maintaining some spare funds will help you cover any unexpected expenses.

    Sources of finance ▽

      After determining your business’ financial requirements, you will need to consider your options in regards to sources of finance and evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of each.

      Some common sources of finance include:

      • Personal savings – usually the easiest and cheapest form of finance, but hold a great degree of personal financial risk.
      • Credit cards – relatively easy to obtain, however, be aware of high interest rates and other charges.
      • Family, friends and relatives – also a cheap and easy form of finance, however, this may potentially affect your relationships.
      • Angel investors – finance is provided in exchange for a share of the business, therefore you will not have complete ownership.
      • Leases – these involve obtaining equipment from financiers, requiring you to make rental payments during the lease.
      • Bank loans – these include term loans, mortgages and bank overdrafts. They can be more difficult to obtain and you will have to meet various requirements, but they are secure.

      If you intend to apply for a loan from a bank or lender, it’s important to shop around, assess the various interest rates, terms and fees, and select the best option for your circumstances. It’s also a good idea to look out for special offers and don’t be afraid to negotiate – the market for finance is very competitive so it’s worthwhile pushing hard to minimise your lending costs.

    Preparing budgets ▽

      A budget allows you to plan, control and make decisions related to the revenue, resources and financial commitments of your business. Without a budget, your business runs the risk of spending more money than it generates in revenue, or not spending enough to allow your micro-business to grow.

      There are many different types of budgets that can be prepared for businesses. It is important to ensure you prepare budgets that best reflect your business’ needs.

      The following points list the main types of budgets and the reasons why they are important:

      • Sales budget – to provide details with regards to the expected sales for the budgeted period.
      • Production budget – to describe how many units must be produced in order to meet the budgeted sales.
      • Expense budget – to estimate all the costs (including material, labour, overhead, and other operating costs) that are likely to be incurred in meeting the budgeted sales targets.
      • Cash flow budget – to forecast all the future cash inflows and outflows for the budgeted period.
      • Budgeted profit and loss statement – to estimate projected sales, expenses and net income (or loss) for the budgeted period.
      • Budgeted balance sheet – to predict the values of business’ assets, liabilities and equity at the end of the budgeted period.

SECTION 2 Financial record keeping ▽

    Keeping daily financial records ▽

      Good record keeping is an important part of monitoring business performance. It also makes it easier for small business owners to meet their taxation obligations. Appropriate and up-to-date financial records provide the necessary information for managing the business efficiently and making sound business decisions.

      To help you maintain your daily financial records, you should consider:

      • Setting up either a manual or electronic record keeping system that suits your needs,
      • Recording your business transactions accurately and promptly,
      • Preparing a summary account (including income and expenditure) at the end of each month.

      Maintaining good financial records starts with a good system and well-organised business records. The system can be a simple one and does not need to be complicated.

      The following are some tips to help you keep good records;

      • Keep your personal affairs separate from your business affairs,
      • Acquire a filing cabinet, hanging folders and some manila folders to help keep your financial papers  organised,
      • Divide and sort your financial records chronologically
      • All incoming documents (such as receipts and invoices) should be kept and filed under the proper headings. Never throw anything out, it might become important in the future.

    Basic Accounting ▽

      To help you maintain good financial records and make sound business decisions, it is important to have a basic understanding of accounting and bookkeeping.

      Basic accounting involves recording business events as they occur and then gathering those events into one place, such as in a Profit & Loss Statement or a Balance Sheet.

      The following are some basic accounting concepts you should know;

      • Accounting cycle – involves analysing a business event/transaction, recording and posting those transactions to ledger accounts, and gathering those accounts together to produce a financial statement.
      • Accounts – the records maintained for the individual assets, liabilities, equity, income and expenses.There are five basic types of accounts, including:
        1. Assets – what the business has or owns (cash, accounts receivable, supplies, equipment)
        2. Liabilities – what the business owes to outsiders (payable, overdrafts, loans)
        3. Owner’s equity – what the owner owns (investment and business profit)
        4. Income  – income earned by the business (sales, fee revenues)
        5. Expenses – costs and expenditure incurred in the operation of the business (purchase, rent, salary and wages, electricity)
      • Debits and Credits – a balancing mechanism in the accounting system. Depending on the type of account that is affected, a debit or credit will either increase or decrease the account balance.

    Maintain a journal system ▽

      A business’ financial records are recorded when a business transaction or event takes place. These business transactions may include a purchase of goods for sales, payment of electricity or phone bills, sales of goods to customers, etc. These transactions are usually supported by documents such as sales slips, cash register tapes, cheque butts, invoices, receipts or adjustment notes.

      Recording daily business transactions is the preliminary step in the accounting cycle, known as “journalising” (i.e. making a journal entry). Each journal entry should have the date of the transaction, a brief description of it, the accounts affected and the amount of money involved. Before the entry is made, you need to make the following decisions:

      • which accounts are affected by the transaction, and
      • which account will be debited and which will be credited.

      For example, on 05 Jul 2010, the business received $70 in cash for computer repair services.

      The affected accounts are: 1) Cash and 2) Service revenue. This transaction results in an increase in cash of the business; therefore “Cash Account” is debited. On the other hand, “Fees revenue account” is to be credited as it is an increase in revenue. (You may need to revisit fundamental rule of Debit vs. Credit).

      On 12 Jul 2010, the business paid electricity bill ($125) for July.

      Most small businesses use subsidiary journals for specific, frequently occurring types of transactions, such as sales, purchases, cash receipts and cash payments journals.     Financial Statements ▽

      To run a successful business, it is important to have a general idea of how your business is doing at a particular time. For example, you will need to know where your cash is coming from and going to, how much money your customers owe you, and how much it costs to run your operation.

      The financial statements will help you answer all these questions. The three basic financial statements include;

      Income statement – a report that shows your profit or loss for a specified period of time (such as a month). It should contain all your revenue and expenditure for that specified period of time.

      Balance sheet – a report indicating the value of your business at a specific point in time. This statement shows all your business’s assets, liabilities and your equity.

      Cash flow statement – a report that shows cash activities for a specified period of time. This report is one of the most crucial reports for small businesses, as it helps the business owner keep track of the flow of their cash and decide how they can use their cash better.

SECTION 3 Financial management ▽

    Accounts payable and receivable ▽

      Accounts payable (AP) and accounts receivable (AR) both deal with business credit. The difference is that AP deals with the businesses that you owe, while AR is about the businesses or individuals that owe you.

      Maintaining your AP is about managing bills that you have to pay. These include product purchases, services or supplies used in the business, payroll and taxes and other items such as utility, internet, insurance or repair bills.

      To help you effectively manage your AP, consider the following;

      • Keep your unpaid bills separate and prioritise them according to date and value.
      • Use direct-debit or auto-charge services for recurring payments.
      • Maintain consistency by making payments on the same date.
      • Keep a record of all payments and file paid bills promptly to avoid paying twice.

      Handling AR, on the other hand, is about managing and collecting the money owed to your business from your customers. This can prove to be a more difficult process, particularly when dealing with collecting overdue accounts.

      To handle your AR effectively, you will need to consider the following factors when setting up your AR process:

      • Properly maintaining customer details and credit information.
      • Appropriate credit terms and billing cycle – remember the faster you bill, the faster you will get paid.
      • Providing small discounts to encourage early payment.
      • Developing and maintaining age receivable schedules,
      • Regularly review age receivables and monthly customer statements.
      • Implement policies to ensure timely and efficient collection of outstanding accounts, such as making follow up phone calls or setting reminders.

      To help you maintain a steady cash flow and deal effectively with business credit, both accounts receivable and payable should be recorded and paid when due.

    Monitoring cash control ▽

      One of the most crucial aspects in running a successful small business is having sufficient cash to meet the day to day running costs of the business. Without cash, even a profitable business will not survive if it doesn’t have enough cash to pay the bills.

      To help you maintain a positive, smooth-running level of cash flow, consider the following;

      Stop all unnecessary spending – making money is all about ensuring you generate more cash than you spend. A good way to start is to minimise your spending, keep track of all your expenses (regardless of size) ensure that every purchase you make is necessary.

      Actively track your expenditures and income – you may decide to track these daily, weekly or fortnightly depending on the volume of your business, but the more frequently you track them, the better. A simple system using spreadsheets can be a great tool to help you keep track of your spending and income.

      Develop a business budget – having a budget in place is a good starting point to understand your cash position. Realistic budgets help you make accurate predictions of your income and expenditures and also allow you to track the difference between what you expected and what actually occurred. Most small business owners find budgets to be a great tool to help avoid wasteful spending and improve profitability.

    Reconcile a bank statement ▽

      A bank reconciliation is a way of comparing and matching the entries in your cash book against those appearing on your bank statement to determine the adjusted cash balance. It will help you to identify errors in your records and highlight any banking errors.

      The following steps will help you prepare a simple bank reconciliation:

      1. Make sure your cash book is as up to date as possible.
      2. Compare receipts in your cash book with the deposits (credits) listed on your bank statement and tick off the items that appear in both.
      3. Compare payments in your cash book with the withdrawals (debits) listed on your bank statement and tick off the items that appear in both.
      4. Update your cash book with any un-ticked items that appear on your bank statement but are not included in your cash book.
      5. Balance your cash book to produce the adjusted cash balance.
      6. Add/ deduct any un-ticked receipts/ payments on your cash book to/ from the ending cash balance as per the bank statement.
      7. Now that all the outstanding items have been added or deducted, the recalculated balance as per bank statement (from step 6) should match with the adjusted balance as per the cash book (from step 5). If not, check your cash book as well as the bank statement for recording errors and take appropriate action to rectify those errors.

SECTION 4 Monitoring business performance ▽

    Income and expenditure ▽

      A simple and effective way to monitor the performance of your micro-business is to review its income and expenses regularly.

      Income

      Income represents the total amount of money that your business earns or derives within a certain period of time. It includes:

      • Money received from selling goods and services
      • Interest earned on savings
      • Dividends on investments
      • Rebates your business receives
      • Rent due to be paid to your business

      Expenses

      Expenses are all the business outlays for the period, excluding any drawings by the business owner. These include:

      • Cost of goods sold – material, labour and overheads attributable to the production of goods sold by a business.
      • Fixed expenses – rent, insurance, licensee fees, etc.
      • Variable expenses – advertising, delivery charges, electricity, etc.

      If income exceeds business expenses, the business will have effectively made a profit. On the other hand, if expenses exceed income, a loss would have been made.

      Depending on the nature, size, and scale of your business’ operation, you may need to review its income and expenditures regularly (i.e. weekly, monthly, quarterly, half yearly or yearly). It will help you to understand your current level of performance and assist you with formulating strategies for the future.

    Business performance ▽

      To assess the performance of your micro-business, you need to develop a set of key performance indicators (KPI’s). KPI’s are metrics used to help a business define and measure progress towards achieving its objectives and success factors. They can be expressed in either financial or non-financial terms and reflect the nature of your business.

      KPI’s vary from business to business and depend on what the overall goals of the business are, however, some of the common ones are:

      • Sales volume
      • Sales growth
      • Profit margin/ percentage of loss
      • Customer base
      • Market share
      • Rate and volume of production
      • Product performance and quality
      • Return on investment

      In addition to KPI’s, you also need to consider factors that indicate your business is achieving success. Critical success factors are business goals that must be achieved in order for the business to be consider successful.

      Some common critical success factors include:

      • Customer value
      • Customer satisfaction
      • Operational efficiency
      • Cost effectiveness
      • Low staff turnover.

    Assessing financial viability ▽

      Financial viability can be defined as a business’ ability to generate sufficient income to meet its operating expenses and financial obligations, as well as providing the potential for future growth.

      Reviewing the current performance level and financial position of your business and considering the future will allow you to determine its financial viability.

      You should consider the following questions when assessing the financial viability of your micro-business:

      • Is your business currently performing better or worse than it has in the recent past?
      • Is your business generating enough income to support your personal needs?
      • Can you always find the cash to pay your creditors on time?
      • Do you have any outstanding debts owed to your by debtors?
      • Do you find that you need to borrow money to keep the business going?
      • Could your business survive if unexpected events occurred that negatively impacted sales?
      • If your business is not currently profitable, do you see it becoming so in the future?
      • How long can you continue operating whilst making a loss?
      • Considering all of the money, resources and effort you put into operating your business, do you feel that it is worthwhile continuing into the future?

    Professional advice ▽

      Business owners usually acquire a general level of knowledge about running a business by operating their own venture. However, their knowledge may be limited and may not be sufficient to effectively meet the complex needs of operating a successful business. Therefore, it can be helpful to seek advice and assistance from professional advisors.

      Professional business advisors work with their clients’ businesses to provide specialised skills and knowledge in specific aspects of business operation. They also assist in ensuring that all of the business’ obligations, requirements and entitlements are being met, and can help them in obtaining and managing their financial resources.

      Some of the most common professional advisors include:

      • Accountants – offer advice and services on business planning, record keeping, preparation of financial statements, taxation compliance, estimation of financial projections and determining future funding requirements.
      • Bankers – provide advice and assistance on loan facilities, loan products, loan applications, cash flow management, risk management, money management, investment options, etc.
      • Solicitors/ lawyers – offer legal services relating to issues such as contracts, leases, taxation, disputes, litigation, intellectual property, debt recovery and so forth.
      • Insurance brokers – give advice on risk identification, risk assessment, risk minimisation, cost-effective insurance options, lodging insurance claims, etc.
      • Financial planners – assist with developing financial strategies, tax planning, retirement planning and investment advice.
      • Management consultants – advise on areas including human resource management, training, marketing and operation.

      In addition to these professional business advisors, you can also obtain advice from government agencies and advisory services. Many provide free information and assistance covering a range of business issues including business planning, finance, marketing, employment, legal and regulatory compliance, etc.
